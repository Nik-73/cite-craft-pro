## I. CONSTITUTIONAL LAW & THEORY

### 1. **The Separation-of-Powers Counterrevolution**
**Authors**: Nikolas Bowie & Daphna Renan (Harvard Law School)
**Citation**: 131 Yale L.J. 2020 (2022)
**Awards**: 2023 ABA Administrative Law Section Annual Scholarship Award

**Brief**: This radical reinterpretation argues that modern separation-of-powers doctrine emerged from post-Reconstruction revanchism and the violent reassertion of white Southern power. The 'juristocratic' model was constructed to limit democratic federal power expansion during Reconstruction.

**Why Exceptional**: Fundamentally challenges how courts and scholars theorize constitutional governance by exposing the racial origins of separation-of-powers doctrine.

**Key Insights**:
- The juristocratic model was designed to limit democratic expansion during/after Reconstruction
- Both originalism and living constitutionalism selectively use historical narratives to legitimize interpretations
- Constitutional change is embedded in political conflict and power dynamics, not neutral legal principle

**Ratings**: Importance 9 | Novelty 9 | Scholarship 9

### 2. **Determining Rights**
**Author**: Jud Campbell (Stanford Law School)
**Citation**: 138 Harvard L. Rev. 921 (2025)

**Brief**: Challenges contemporary constitutional theory by demonstrating that Founding-era practitioners grounded rights in natural and customary law rather than textual enumeration. Determining rights content was treated as political, not judicial. The Bill of Rights was declaratory, not creative.

**Why Exceptional**: Originalist methodology leads to deeply non-originalist conclusions, undermining jurisprudential consensus about how rights obtain determinate meaning.

**Key Insights**:
- Founding generation understood rights as customary/natural, not textual in origin
- Rights required popular (political) determination of content rather than judicial interpretation
- Eighteenth-century rights enumeration was declaratory and clarificatory, not limitative

**Ratings**: Importance 9 | Novelty 10 | Scholarship 10

### 3. **Comstockery: How Government Censorship Gave Birth to Sexual and Reproductive Freedom**
**Authors**: Reva B. Siegel & Mary Ziegler (Yale/UC Davis Law Schools)
**Citation**: 134 Yale L.J. 1071 (2025)

**Brief**: First comprehensive legal history of the Comstock Act (1873-present), revealing how its obscurity law origins were selectively invoked post-Dobbs as an anti-abortion tool. The statute's revival represents "constitutional revivalism" through strategic historical amnesia.

**Why Exceptional**: Catalyzed major online symposium; demonstrates how legal terminology can obscure/illuminate injustices; shows constitutional interpretation shaped by strategic forgetting.

**Key Insights**:
- Comstock Act's original purpose (postal obscenity law) bears little resemblance to anti-abortion revival claims
- Modern constitutional interpretation shaped by strategic historical forgetting and selective memory
- Roots of modern free-speech and reproductive liberty emerge from suppressed constitutional tradition

**Ratings**: Importance 10 | Novelty 10 | Scholarship 9

### 5. **Our Marbury: Loper Bright and the Administrative State**
**Author**: Cass R. Sunstein (Harvard Law School)
**Citation**: 74 Duke L.J. 1893 (2025)

**Brief**: Interprets Loper Bright (overruling Chevron) as administrative law's "Marbury moment"—the defining statement of judicial supremacy in interpreting law. Argues practical impact may be narrower than critics predict, but symbolic significance is profound.

**Why Exceptional**: From the nation's preeminent administrative law scholar; published immediately after decision; definitive interpretation shaping discourse about administrative law's future.

**Key Insights**:
- Loper Bright's significance lies in "green light" to courts rather than fundamental doctrinal alteration
- Chevron already resulted in substantial invalidations; Loper Bright increases rate, not revolution
- Reflects constitutional interpretation about judiciary's role transcending specific deference doctrine

## III. CRIMINAL LAW & PROCEDURE

### 7. **Unwarranted Warrants? An Empirical Analysis of Judicial Review**
**Authors**: Miguel F.P. de Figueiredo, Brett Hashimoto & Dane Thorley
**Citation**: 138 Harv. L. Rev. 1959 (2025)

**Brief**: Largest quantitative empirical study of warrants ever conducted—analyzing 33,000+ warrant applications through Utah's e-Warrants system over three years. Reveals shocking findings: 98% approval rate, 93% approved on first submission, median 3-minute review, 10% approved in under 60 seconds.

**Why Exceptional**: Unprecedented empirical scale; challenges conventional assumptions about judicial oversight; reveals systemic rubber-stamping.

**Key Insights**:
- Judicial warrant review is performative rather than substantive despite constitutional mandate
- 60-second approvals suggest judges not meaningfully evaluating Fourth Amendment compliance
- Massive dataset provides unprecedented empirical evidence for warrant procedure reform
- Data-driven approach marks methodological innovation in Fourth Amendment scholarship

**Ratings**: Importance 10 | Novelty 10 | Scholarship 10

### 9. **Inside the Black Box of Prosecutor Discretion**
**Authors**: Megan S. Wright, Shima Baradaran Baughman & Christopher T. Robertson
**Citation**: 55 U.C. Davis L. Rev. 2133 (2022)

**Brief**: Recruited 500+ prosecutors nationwide to charge identical cases under identical law, revealing stunning heterogeneity—some dismissing while others demand years of incarceration. Exposes largely unconstrained prosecutorial discretion as primary driver of mass incarceration.

**Why Exceptional**: Experimental methodology confirms wild heterogeneity; prosecutor decisions vary by prosecutor more than case characteristics; demonstrates systemic lack of standards.

**Key Insights**:
- Experimental design demonstrates two prosecutors with identical facts reach dramatically different outcomes
- Prosecutors showed concerning severity in charging relatively minor cases
- Decision-making varies by prosecutor ideology/preferences rather than case characteristics
- Evidence of lack of internal guidelines constraining charging decisions

**Ratings**: Importance 10 | Novelty 10 | Scholarship 10

## VI. LEGAL THEORY & JURISPRUDENCE

### 16. **The Amended Statute**
**Author**: Jesse M. Cross
**Citation**: 92 U. Chi. L. Rev. 1 (2025)

**Brief**: Documents nearly 500,000 amendments to federal statutes since Founding through 700+ Statutes at Large searches. Argues U.S. has become "republic of amended statutes," fundamentally reconceptualizing statutory interpretation theory. Amendatory patterns enabled textualism's rise while rendering methodological originalism impossible.

**Why Exceptional**: First comprehensive empirical documentation of federal statutory amendments; fundamentally reframes statutory interpretation doctrine and legislative history analysis.

**Key Insights**:
- Statutory amendments (tens of thousands per Congress) constitute actual democratic text of modern law
- Amendatory story behind Supreme Court cases reveals Court's inability to locate democratic decisions
- Textualism's rise intrinsically linked to textual stability from consolidated collections
- True originalism becomes methodologically impossible with extensive amendment histories

**Ratings**: Importance 10 | Novelty 10 | Scholarship 10

---

### 17. **Constitutional Rights as Protected Reasons**
**Author**: Stephanie Hall Barclay
**Citation**: 92 U. Chi. L. Rev. 1347 (2025)

**Brief**: Proposes novel framework understanding constitutional rights as "protected reasons" drawing from Joseph Raz's legal philosophy. Rights operate as both first-order reasons for government protection and second-order exclusionary reasons preventing reliance on competing considerations.

**Why Exceptional**: Paradigm shift in constitutional theory; first philosophically rigorous and institutionally realistic account of how rights function across three branches.

**Key Insights**:
- Existing constitutional rights theories incoherent or incomplete; protected reasons framework provides missing coherence
- Elegantly addresses tension between individual rights protection and democratic self-governance
- Understanding rights as protected reasons aligns theory with institutional reality
- Different branches have different competencies in identifying interests vs. evaluating competing goods

**Ratings**: Importance 10 | Novelty 9 | Scholarship 10

**Key Insights**:

- Rather than seeking new "AI law," existing doctrinal categories sufficient with thoughtful application
- Legal framing choices are not neutral technical matters but fundamentally constitute AI as legal object

### 30. **Inverse Integration and the Relational Deficit of Disability Rights Law**
**Author**: Yaron Covo
**Citation**: 124 Colum. L. Rev. 3 (April 2024)

**Brief**: Reconceptualizes disability integration by introducing "inverse integration"—when nondisabled persons voluntarily enter disability-focused spaces. - Inverse integration demonstrates disability-specific spaces can be inherently valuable rather than segregationist
- Reframes disability integration from rights issue to relational issue
- Fostering disabled/nondisabled relationships should be core goal in increasingly isolated society
