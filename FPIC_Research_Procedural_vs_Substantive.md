# Comprehensive Legal Research: FPIC as a Procedural Duty to Consult, Not a Substantive Right to Veto

## Executive Summary

This research compiles legal scholarship, jurisprudence, and international documents supporting the argument that Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) is fundamentally a **procedural duty to consult in good faith**, rather than a substantive right conferring veto power over development projects. The sources demonstrate a consistent international legal interpretation that FPIC requires robust consultation processes and meaningful participation, but does not create an absolute right to prevent state action.

---

## 1. LEGAL DISTINCTION BETWEEN CONSULTATION AND CONSENT

### 1.1 Kebaowek First Nation v Canadian Nuclear Laboratories, 2025 FC 319 (Federal Court of Canada)

**Citation:** Kebaowek First Nation v Canadian Nuclear Laboratories, 2025 FC 319

**Legal Reasoning:** The Federal Court of Canada provided one of the clearest articulations that FPIC does not amount to a veto power. The Court quashed a decision of the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission, clarifying that while FPIC is not a veto or a right to a particular outcome, it is a right to a robust process directed towards finding mutual agreement. The Court concluded that, consistent with international scholarship, FPIC was not intended to be a general "veto power." This case represents the most explicit Canadian judicial statement on the procedural nature of FPIC under UNDRIP.

**Key Quotes:**
- "FPIC is not a veto or a right to a particular outcome, it is a right to a robust process directed towards finding mutual agreement"
- "Consistent with international scholarship, FPIC was not intended to be a general 'veto power'"
- "FPIC does not amount to a veto over decision-making"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This decision aligns with international interpretations from ILO supervisory bodies and UN Special Rapporteur positions that emphasize consultation as a process rather than an outcome-determinative right.

### 1.2 ILO Convention 169: CEACR General Observations (2008, 2010, 2018)

**Citation:** Committee of Experts on the Application of Conventions and Recommendations (CEACR), General Observations on Convention No. 169 (2008, 2010, 2018)

**Legal Reasoning:** The ILO's expert supervisory body has consistently clarified that Convention 169 distinguishes between consultation (the general requirement) and consent (required only in specific circumstances like relocation). The CEACR emphasized that consultation must be formal, full, and exercised in good faith, with genuine dialogue characterized by communication, understanding, and mutual respect. However, the CEACR explicitly stated that consultations do not imply a right to veto, and the result of consultations is not necessarily the reaching of agreement or consent. The Committee has repeatedly stated that obtaining agreement or consent is the purpose of the consultation process, not an independent requirement.

**Key Quotes:**
- "ILO Convention 169 does not provide indigenous peoples with a veto right, as obtaining the agreement or consent is the purpose of engaging in the consultation process, and is not an independent requirement"
- "Consultations do not imply a right to veto nor is the result of the consultations necessarily the reaching of agreement or consent"
- "Pro forma consultations or mere information will not meet the requirements of the Convention"
- "The Committee emphasized the importance of ensuring the right of indigenous and tribal peoples to decide their development priorities through meaningful and effective consultation"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** These observations form the authoritative interpretation of the most widely ratified binding international treaty on Indigenous rights, establishing the baseline that consultation is distinct from consent in international law.

### 1.3 S.J. Rombouts, "Consultation and Consent Norms under ILO Convention No. 169 and the UN Declaration" (EMRIP Submission)

**Citation:** S.J. Rombouts, "Consultation and consent norms under ILO convention no. 169 and the UN declaration on the rights of indigenous peoples compared: Contribution to the United Nations expert mechanism on the rights of indigenous peoples' (EMRIP) annual thematic study," Written submission to the EMRIP expert seminar on free, prior and informed consent, Available at: https://www.ohchr.org/sites/default/files/Documents/Issues/IPeoples/EMRIP/FPIC/RomboutsSJ.pdf

**Legal Reasoning:** This comprehensive scholarly analysis compares consultation and consent norms across ILO Convention 169 and UNDRIP. Rombouts demonstrates that Convention 169 uses different terminology for different situations: "consultation and participation" as the general requirement; "cooperation" in certain contexts; obligations not to take measures contrary to "freely-expressed wishes"; and "free and informed consent" only where relocation is necessary. The paper establishes that consent requirements in C169 are limited to specific exceptional circumstances, while consultation is the general procedural safeguard. This textual analysis shows the deliberate distinction between these concepts in international law.

**Key Quotes:**
- "ILO Convention 169 does not provide indigenous peoples with a veto right, as obtaining the agreement or consent is the purpose of engaging in the consultation process, and is not an independent requirement"
- "Convention No. 169 uses terms such as the obligation to 'cooperate' with indigenous peoples; the obligation not to take measures contrary to the 'freely-expressed wishes' of indigenous peoples; and the obligation to seek 'free and informed consent' of indigenous peoples where 'relocation…is considered necessary'"
- "During its redrafting, Indigenous groups argued for the inclusion of the terms 'consent and control' in reference to Indigenous peoples' input on activities that might impact their rights, but government and business representatives did not agree and offered instead the terms 'consultation and participation'"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This analysis provides crucial historical context showing that the international community deliberately chose "consultation" over "consent" as the general standard, reserving consent for exceptional circumstances.

### 1.4 Accountability Framework Initiative, "Operational Guidance on Free, Prior and Informed Consent" (2019)

**Citation:** Accountability Framework Initiative, "Operational Guidance on Free, Prior and Informed Consent," June 2019, Available at: https://accountability-framework.org/fileadmin/uploads/afi/Documents/Operational_Guidance/OG_FPIC-2020-5.pdf

**Legal Reasoning:** This operational guidance, developed by a coalition of environmental and social organizations, clarifies that FPIC is fundamentally a procedural safeguard, not a stand-alone substantive right. The guidance explains that "the duty to consult and the requirement to seek FPIC are grounded in procedural safeguards of substantive rights" and that "FPIC is neither an 'end in itself' nor a 'stand-alone' right per se but a derivative of the underlying substantive rights which it is designed to protect." This framing emphasizes that FPIC serves as a mechanism to protect substantive rights to land, resources, and self-determination, rather than constituting an independent veto power. The procedural nature means it requires good faith processes but not necessarily consent as an outcome.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The duty to consult and the requirement to seek FPIC are grounded in procedural safeguards of substantive rights"
- "FPIC is neither an 'end in itself' nor a 'stand-alone' right per se but a derivative of the underlying substantive rights which it is designed to protect"
- "FPIC builds on and goes beyond the legal duty to consult"
- "As a process, FPIC is a series of information exchanges, consultation, internal deliberation, and negotiation steps conducted to seek consent from the affected Indigenous Peoples/Local Communities"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This interpretation aligns with the understanding that FPIC is a means of protecting substantive rights rather than an absolute bar to development, consistent with ILO and UN interpretations.

### 1.5 Tara Ward, "The Right to Free, Prior, and Informed Consent: Indigenous Peoples' Participation Rights within International Law"

**Citation:** Tara Ward, "The Right to Free, Prior, and Informed Consent: Indigenous Peoples' Participation Rights within International Law," Northwestern Journal of International Human Rights, Vol. 10, Issue 2, Article 2 (2011), Available at: https://scholarlycommons.law.northwestern.edu/njihr/vol10/iss2/2/

**Legal Reasoning:** Ward's influential article argues that while FPIC represents an important advancement in Indigenous rights, international law has not yet crystallized a right to absolute consent functioning as a veto. The article examines state practice and opinio juris, concluding that "as of yet, the right to full FPIC is not part of customary international law, though there is a well-defined consensus that States have an obligation to consult with Indigenous peoples in good faith regarding projects within their lands." Ward notes that the question of whether FPIC constitutes a full veto right is particularly contentious within international law, with some scholars arguing that FPIC is meant to ensure meaningful participation rather than providing veto power. The article emphasizes that FPIC requirements exist on a spectrum, with consultation being the baseline and consent required only in exceptional circumstances.

**Key Quotes:**
- "As of yet, the right to full FPIC is not part of customary international law, though there is a well-defined consensus that States have an obligation to consult with Indigenous peoples in good faith"
- "The question of whether FPIC constitutes a full veto right for affected peoples is particularly contentious within international law"
- "Some scholars argue that FPIC is not meant to be a veto right, but rather a way of ensuring that Indigenous peoples meaningfully participate in decisions directly impacting their lands, territories, and resources"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This scholarly analysis accurately reflects the current state of international law, where consultation duties are well-established but absolute consent requirements remain contested and limited to specific circumstances.

## 2. CASE LAW AND DECISIONS DISTINGUISHING CONSULTATION FROM CONSENT

### 2.1 Saramaka People v. Suriname, Inter-American Court of Human Rights, Series C No. 172 (2007)

**Citation:** Case of the Saramaka People v. Suriname, Inter-American Court of Human Rights (Ser. C) No. 172 (November 28, 2007)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Saramaka decision is a landmark case that established a crucial distinction between consultation and consent based on the magnitude of impact. The Court held that while Suriname should have consulted the Saramaka "at the early stages of a development or investment plan," full consent is required only in specific circumstances. The Court stated: "when it comes to development plans or large-scale investment that would have a major impact within [the indigenous community's] territory, the state has an obligation not only to consult [the indigenous group], but also to obtain the free, prior and informed consent according to their customs and traditions." This creates a two-tier framework: general consultation for most projects, and consent only for major impacts. Significantly, the Court's framework still places the duty on states to engage in consultation processes, with consent being an elevated requirement for particularly significant projects rather than a universal requirement.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Suriname should have consulted the Saramaka 'at the early stages of a development or investment plan'"
- "When it comes to development plans or large-scale investment that would have a major impact within [the indigenous community's] territory, the state has an obligation not only to consult [the indigenous group], but also to obtain the free, prior and informed consent"
- The Court held that the Saramakas "had certain resemblances with traditional indigenous communities and therefore enjoyed the same rights"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This decision influenced subsequent Inter-American Court cases and has been cited widely. It establishes that even in a human rights court context, consent is required only when impacts meet a "major impact" threshold, not as a general requirement. The consultation duty applies broadly, while consent is reserved for exceptional circumstances.

### 2.2 Kichwa Indigenous People of Sarayaku v. Ecuador, Inter-American Court of Human Rights, Series C No. 245 (2012)

**Citation:** Case of the Kichwa Indigenous People of Sarayaku v. Ecuador, Inter-American Court of Human Rights (Ser. C) No. 245 (June 27, 2012)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Sarayaku decision built upon Saramaka but notably spoke strictly in terms of consultation rather than consent, despite finding significant impacts on the Indigenous community. The Court held that "the duty to consult with indigenous communities about legal or administrative measures that will affect them directly has become a general principle of international law." The ruling emphasized that Ecuador had failed to conduct proper consultation when it granted oil exploration rights without adequate engagement with the Sarayaku People. Interestingly, despite the Court finding that Ecuador had failed to protect the Sarayaku Peoples' right to life and physical integrity due to explosives left by oil companies, it framed the remedy in terms of consultation obligations rather than requiring consent. This demonstrates that even where impacts are severe, international courts frame the primary duty as consultation, with consent being an exceptional requirement.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The duty to consult with indigenous communities about legal or administrative measures that will affect them directly has become a general principle of international law"
- "The ruling said that whenever large-scale development will have a significant impact within Indigenous territory, the state has the obligation to obtain FPIC with respect for their culture and traditions"
- The Court "speaks strictly in terms of consultation rather than consent, despite finding that Ecuador had failed to protect the Sarayaku Peoples' right to life and physical integrity"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This case shows that even the Inter-American Court, which has been most progressive on Indigenous rights, maintains a distinction between consultation (the general duty) and consent (required for major impacts). The tension between the Saramaka "major impact" test and Sarayaku's consultation-focused remedy suggests international law favors procedural consultation requirements.

### 2.3 Haida Nation v. British Columbia (Minister of Forests), [2004] 3 S.C.R. 511 (Supreme Court of Canada)

**Citation:** Haida Nation v. British Columbia (Minister of Forests), [2004] 3 S.C.R. 511, 2004 SCC 73

**Legal Reasoning:** Haida Nation is the leading Supreme Court of Canada decision establishing the constitutional duty to consult Aboriginal peoples. The Court explicitly framed this as a procedural duty rather than a substantive veto right. The Court held that "the Crown has a duty to consult Indigenous peoples when it intends to act in a manner that may adversely affect potential or established Aboriginal or treaty rights," and that "the Crown's duty to consult with Aboriginal peoples is a procedural duty grounded upon the honour of the Crown, being both legal and constitutional in nature." Critically, the Court established that consultation exists on a spectrum proportionate to the strength of the Aboriginal claim and the severity of potential impacts. The Court stated that the duty may range from minimal notice and information-sharing for weak claims with minor impacts, to deep consultation and accommodation for strong claims with significant impacts. However, even at the highest end of the spectrum, the duty does not provide a veto—the Crown may proceed after meaningful consultation and reasonable accommodation, even without Indigenous consent.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The Crown has a duty to consult Indigenous peoples when it intends to act in a manner that may adversely affect potential or established Aboriginal or treaty rights"
- "The Crown's duty to consult with Aboriginal peoples is a procedural duty grounded upon the honour of the Crown, being both legal and constitutional in nature"
- "The scope of the duty to consult is proportionate to a preliminary assessment of the strength of the case supporting the existence of the right or title, and to the seriousness of the potentially adverse effect"
- "The duty to consult arises when the Crown has actual or constructive knowledge of the potential existence of Aboriginal rights or title and contemplates conduct that might adversely affect those rights or title"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Haida spectrum approach has been influential internationally and aligns with the ILO Convention 169 framework of proportionate consultation. By establishing consultation as a procedural constitutional duty rather than a substantive veto, Canadian jurisprudence supports the interpretation that FPIC in international law is fundamentally about process.

### 2.4 Tsilhqot'in Nation v. British Columbia, [2014] 2 S.C.R. 257 (Supreme Court of Canada)

**Citation:** Tsilhqot'in Nation v. British Columbia, [2014] 2 S.C.R. 257, 2014 SCC 44

**Legal Reasoning:** The Tsilhqot'in decision was the first case in Canadian history to grant a declaration of Aboriginal title. Significantly, even with proven Aboriginal title (not merely asserted or claimed rights), the Court maintained a distinction between consultation requirements and veto rights. The Court held that "where Aboriginal title is established, governments and others seeking to use the land must obtain the consent of the Aboriginal title holders." However, this is not an absolute veto, as the Court clarified that "absent consent, a government must show that: it discharged its procedural duty to consult and accommodate; its actions were in pursuit of a compelling and substantial objective; and the action is consistent with the Crown's fiduciary obligation to the Aboriginal group." This creates a justification framework where the government can proceed without consent if it meets a high threshold of justification. Before title is established, only consultation is required: "the Crown must consult with any Aboriginal groups that assert title to the land about the proposed land uses, and if appropriate, accommodate those groups."

**Key Quotes:**
- "Where Aboriginal title has been claimed but not yet established, there is a spectrum of consultation"
- "Where Aboriginal title is established, governments and others seeking to use the land must obtain the consent of the Aboriginal title holders"
- "Absent consent, a government must show that: it discharged its procedural duty to consult and accommodate; its actions were in pursuit of a compelling and substantial objective; and the action is consistent with the Crown's fiduciary obligation"
- "Before Aboriginal title is established, the Crown must consult with any Aboriginal groups that assert title to the land about the proposed land uses"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This decision demonstrates that even where Indigenous land rights are strongest (proven title), international and domestic law frameworks allow for justified infringement after meaningful consultation, supporting the argument that FPIC is primarily procedural rather than an absolute substantive barrier.

### 2.5 Endorois Welfare Council v. Kenya, African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights, Comm. No. 276/2003 (2009)

**Citation:** Centre for Minority Rights Development (Kenya) and Minority Rights Group International on behalf of Endorois Welfare Council v. Kenya, African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights, Communication No. 276/2003 (November 25, 2009)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Endorois case was a landmark decision by the African Commission finding that Kenya violated the rights of the Endorois people by evicting them from their ancestral lands to create a game reserve without adequate consultation. The Commission held that "participation requires the state to disseminate information and ensure that consultations are done in good faith through culturally appropriate procedures." The African Commission emphasized procedural requirements: states must provide information, ensure culturally appropriate consultation processes, and act in good faith. Notably, while the Commission found violations, it framed the remedy in terms of consultation procedures rather than granting the Endorois an absolute veto over the game reserve. The decision has had limited practical implementation, with Kenya failing to return lands or provide adequate remedies 14 years later, demonstrating the challenges of enforcing even procedural consultation rights.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Participation requires the state to disseminate information and ensure that consultations are done in good faith through culturally appropriate procedures"
- The Commission found Kenya had violated the Endorois rights by evicting them "without adequate consultation"
- The decision emphasized the need for "culturally appropriate procedures" and "good faith" consultation

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Endorois decision aligns with international standards emphasizing the procedural nature of consultation requirements, though it also illustrates the enforcement challenges faced by Indigenous peoples even when procedural rights are violated.

### 2.6 African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights v. Republic of Kenya (Ogiek Case), African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights, Application No. 006/2012 (2017)

**Citation:** African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights v. Republic of Kenya, African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights, Application No. 006/2012 (May 26, 2017)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Ogiek case represents the African Court's first major decision on Indigenous peoples' rights. The Court found that the Ogiek are an Indigenous people of Kenya and that their eviction from the Mau Forest violated their rights under the African Charter. The Court held that "the Kenyan state had failed to 'effectively consult' with the Ogiek" and that "the expulsion of the Ogiek from their land without prior consultation violated their property rights." The Court's framing is significant: it emphasized the failure to consult rather than the lack of consent. The decision does not establish that the Ogiek have a veto over forest management decisions, but rather that they have a right to be meaningfully consulted. Like the Endorois case, implementation has been problematic, with the Kenyan government continuing to deny reparations and refusing to allow the Ogiek to return to their lands even after the Court's decision.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The Court found that the Kenyan state had failed to 'effectively consult' with the Ogiek"
- "The expulsion of the Ogiek from their land without prior consultation violated their property rights"
- The African Court ruled "that the Ogiek are an indigenous people of Kenya and that their eviction from the Mau Forest violated their rights"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Ogiek decision reinforces that international human rights bodies frame Indigenous rights violations primarily in terms of failures of consultation procedure rather than absence of consent, supporting the procedural interpretation of FPIC.

### 2.7 Campo Agua'ẽ of the Ava Guaraní People v. Paraguay, UN Human Rights Committee, CCPR/C/132/D/2552/2015 (2021)

**Citation:** Campo Agua'ẽ of the Ava Guaraní People v. Paraguay, UN Human Rights Committee, CCPR/C/132/D/2552/2015 (October 12, 2021)

**Legal Reasoning:** This decision by the UN Human Rights Committee (CCPR) addressed pesticide contamination of Indigenous lands in Paraguay. The Committee affirmed that under Article 27 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), interpreted in light of UNDRIP, Indigenous peoples have "the inalienable right to enjoy the territories and natural resources that they have traditionally used for their subsistence and cultural identity." The Committee found Paraguay had violated its obligations by failing to adequately consult with the Ava Guaraní people. This was the Committee's first decision to affirm that, for Indigenous peoples, the notion of "home" should be understood within the context of their special relationship with territories. The Committee recommended Paraguay engage in consultation with the affected communities, but did not frame the remedy as requiring Indigenous consent for all activities. This demonstrates that even under human rights treaty obligations, the primary requirement is consultation, with the goal of protecting substantive rights to culture and territory.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Article 27 of the Covenant, interpreted in the light of the [UNDRIP], enshrines the inalienable right of indigenous peoples to enjoy the territories and natural resources that they have traditionally used for their subsistence and cultural identity"
- This is "the Committee's first decision to affirm that, in the case of Indigenous people, the notion of 'home' should be understood within the context of the special relationship between them and their territories"
- The Committee emphasized the failure to conduct adequate consultation

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The CCPR's emphasis on consultation obligations under the ICCPR reinforces that international human rights law protects Indigenous peoples primarily through procedural consultation requirements rather than absolute consent or veto rights.

### 2.8 Raposa Serra do Sol Case, Brazilian Supreme Court, Pet. 3.388 (2009)

**Citation:** Petição 3.388, Brazilian Supreme Federal Tribunal (March 19, 2009)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Raposa Serra do Sol case was a landmark ruling by Brazil's Supreme Court involving a 1.7 million hectare Indigenous reservation home to 20,000 Indigenous people (Makuxi, Wapixana, Ingarikó, Taurepang, and Patamona peoples). The Court upheld the constitutional demarcation of Indigenous lands, saying it had been done according to the constitution and that its size and borders should be maintained. However, the Court also imposed "institutional safeguards" that limited Indigenous autonomy, including establishing the "Marco Temporal" (time frame) criterion suggesting Indigenous peoples only have rights to territories they were in possession of on October 5, 1988 (the date of Brazil's Federal Constitution). While the case recognized Indigenous land rights, it did not establish Indigenous veto power over development. The decision demonstrates judicial balancing of Indigenous territorial rights with state interests, emphasizing that Indigenous rights exist within constitutional frameworks that allow for state regulation and justified infringement. The Marco Temporal concept has been controversial and was later challenged in subsequent litigation.

**Key Quotes:**
- "On 19th March 2009 in a landmark ruling, the majority of Supreme Court judges upheld the Indians' rights to their land, saying it had been demarcated according to the constitution and that its size and borders should be maintained"
- The ruling imposed "a series of conditions called 'institutional safeguards', among them, the criterion of the Marco Temporal"
- "The Time Frame is a political thesis asserting that indigenous peoples would only have rights to their territories if they were in possession of them on October 5, 1988"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** While recognizing Indigenous land rights, the Brazilian Supreme Court's approach demonstrates that even strong Indigenous rights do not translate into veto power, and state interests can be balanced through procedural and substantive limitations.

### 2.9 Niyamgiri/Vedanta Case, Supreme Court of India (Orissa Mining Corporation v. Ministry of Environment and Forest, 2013)

**Citation:** Orissa Mining Corporation Ltd. v. Ministry of Environment and Forest & Others, Supreme Court of India (2013)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Niyamgiri case is one of the most significant Indigenous rights decisions from India's Supreme Court. The Court ruled that the Gram Sabha (Village Assembly) has the authority to determine whether a proposed mining project would affect individual or community rights including cultural and religious rights under India's Forest Rights Act, 2006. The Court defended the Dongria Kondh community's claim against Vedanta Resources, a multinational corporation, by tying cultural rights to land. The Court upheld the rights of Scheduled Tribes and Traditional Forest Dwellers and emphasized their right to protect religious and cultural practices. Significantly, the Court directed fresh Gram Sabha consultations supervised by a judicial officer and held that no clearance could be granted without determining these rights. While this case gave village assemblies significant power, it framed their role as determining impacts on rights rather than having absolute veto power. The Gram Sabhas ultimately rejected the project, but the legal framework was consultation-based rather than establishing an inherent veto right.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The Supreme Court decreed that the Gram Sabha (Village Assembly) has the authority to determine whether a proposed project would affect individual or community rights including cultural and religious rights"
- "The Court defended an indigenous claim against a multinational corporation by tying cultural rights to land"
- "The Court directed fresh Gram Sabha consultations supervised by a judicial officer and held that no clearance could be granted without determining these rights"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Indian Supreme Court's approach aligns with international frameworks requiring meaningful consultation with decision-making power, while maintaining that consultation is the procedural mechanism through which rights are protected, rather than establishing absolute veto power.

### 2.10 Fosen Wind Farm Case (Fosen Vind), Supreme Court of Norway (2021)

**Citation:** HR-2021-1975-S, Norwegian Supreme Court (October 11, 2021)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Norwegian Supreme Court unanimously ruled that wind turbines on the Fosen peninsula violated the human rights of South Sami reindeer herders under Article 27 of the ICCPR. The Court found that the Fosen Vind project violated Article 27's protection of cultural practices, as traditional Sami reindeer herding is a protected form of cultural practice. This was historic as it was the first time affected Sami parties won against a development project in their traditional areas. However, the remedy was framed in terms of rights violations requiring correction rather than establishing a prospective veto right. Significantly, despite the Court's clear ruling, the Norwegian government took no action for 500 days, and the wind turbines continued operating, demonstrating that even where consultation failures are found, practical remedies do not necessarily include removal of projects. The case led to major demonstrations but also highlighted that human rights violations through inadequate consultation do not automatically result in project cancellation.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The Supreme Court unanimously ruled that wind turbines on the Fosen peninsula violated the human rights of South Sami families"
- "The Court found that the Fosen Vind project violated Article 27 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)"
- "Traditional Sami reindeer herding is a form of protected cultural practice"
- "This was the first time that affected Sami parties won in a case concerning a development project in their traditional areas"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Fosen case demonstrates that violations of consultation and cultural rights can be found by courts without necessarily resulting in absolute veto power or project cancellation, reinforcing the procedural nature of consultation duties.

### 2.11 Karasjok Case, Supreme Court of Norway (2024)

**Citation:** Supreme Court of Norway judgment regarding Karasjok (June 2024)

**Legal Reasoning:** In a controversial 6-5 decision, Norway's Supreme Court ruled that the largely Indigenous community of Karasjok does not have collective property rights over a vast area in Norway's northernmost region. The narrow majority decision stated that the Indigenous Sami community could not establish collective ownership rights, contradicting "the Sami legal understanding" of collective ownership according to a political adviser to Norway's Sami Parliament. This case demonstrates limitations on Indigenous property rights claims even in Nordic countries with relatively progressive Indigenous rights frameworks. The Court's decision shows that consultation rights and cultural protections do not automatically translate into property rights or veto power over land use decisions. The decision has been criticized as contrary to international human rights obligations, but it illustrates that domestic courts may interpret Indigenous rights claims narrowly, maintaining state sovereignty over natural resources and land management.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Norway's Supreme Court ruled that the largely Indigenous community of Karasjok does not have collective property rights over a vast area in the country's northernmost region"
- A political adviser to Norway's Sami Parliament said the decision "contradicts the Sami legal understanding" of collective ownership
- The decision was narrow: 6-5

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** While potentially contrary to international human rights standards, this decision reflects ongoing tensions between Indigenous rights claims and state sovereignty, supporting arguments that international law has not established absolute Indigenous veto rights.

### 2.12 Xolobeni Mining Case, High Court of South Africa (North Gauteng) (2018)

**Citation:** Baleni and Others v Minister of Mineral Resources and Others, High Court of South Africa (North Gauteng), Case No. 73768/2016 (November 22, 2018)

**Legal Reasoning:** The South African High Court issued a landmark ruling that government officials must first obtain consent from affected communities before granting mineral rights. Judge Annali Basson declared that "the mineral resources minister must obtain consent from the community prior to granting any mining right." The case involved the UMgungundlovu community (also known as Xolobeni/Amadiba) resisting plans by Australian mining company Transworld Energy and Mineral Resources to mine titanium on their ancestral lands. The Court ruled that "the Minister of Mineral Resources had no legal authority to grant a mining licence unless provisions protecting informal land rights were implemented, including the provision that no person may be deprived of any informal right to land without consent." This case is notable as it required consent, but it is based on South African constitutional protections of property rights and informal land tenure, not international FPIC standards. The decision is specific to South Africa's legal framework protecting informal land rights and does not establish a general international principle requiring consent for all mining projects.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The mineral resources minister must obtain consent from the community prior to granting any mining right"
- Before the ruling, "South Africa's mining laws required companies to consult affected communities, but did not require explicit permission from them to dig—now, these communities 'may not be deprived of their land without their consent'"
- "The Minister of Mineral Resources had no legal authority to grant a mining licence unless provisions protecting informal land rights were implemented"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** While this case required consent, it is based on South African property law protections rather than international FPIC standards. It demonstrates that consent requirements can arise from specific domestic legal frameworks protecting land tenure, but does not establish that FPIC under international law constitutes a general consent requirement.

## 3. ANALYSIS OF UNDRIP ARTICLE 19

### 3.1 Canadian Government Position on UNDRIP Article 19 (Bill C-15 Senate Testimony, 2021)

**Citation:** "Appearance before the Standing Senate Committee on Aboriginal Peoples: Bill C-15, An Act respecting the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples," May 31, 2021, Available at: https://www.rcaanc-cirnac.gc.ca/eng/1624906622343/1624906649093

**Legal Reasoning:** During testimony on Bill C-15 (Canada's UNDRIP implementation legislation), Canadian government officials clarified their interpretation of Article 19's requirement to "obtain" FPIC. UN representatives and Canadian political leadership endorsed by the government emphasized that "in articles 19 and 32, the emphasis is not on the consent or achieving the consent, it's in the process of working towards achieving consent." The government's position was that "the goal should be to work toward achieving consent with Indigenous people, but if no consent is reached, this doesn't necessarily mean nothing can go forward." This interpretation was supported by noting that "most academics and Indigenous leaders are adamant that FPIC is not a veto power because that implies that it is an absolute power. Human rights are generally not absolute; they are relative to one another and must be balanced." The government relied on international scholarship supporting the view that Article 19 establishes a robust procedural requirement rather than an absolute substantive veto.

**Key Quotes:**
- "In articles 19 and 32, the emphasis is not on the consent or achieving the consent, it's in the process of working towards achieving consent"
- "The goal should be to work toward achieving consent with Indigenous people, but if no consent is reached, this doesn't necessarily mean nothing can go forward"
- "Most academics and Indigenous leaders are adamant that FPIC is not a veto power because that implies that it is an absolute power. Human rights are generally not absolute; they are relative to one another and must be balanced"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This governmental interpretation aligns with ILO Convention 169 supervisory body positions and state practice showing that "obtaining" FPIC is interpreted as engaging in processes aimed at securing consent, not as an absolute requirement for consent as an outcome.

### 3.2 Papillon and Rodon, "Free, Prior and Informed Consent: Between Legal Ambiguity and Political Agency"

**Citation:** Martin Papillon and Thierry Rodon, "Free, Prior and Informed Consent: Between Legal Ambiguity and Political Agency," International Journal on Minority and Group Rights, Vol. 27, Issue 2 (2020): 223-248

### 3.5 UN Declaration Article 19 Text and Context

**Citation:** United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, G.A. Res. 61/295, U.N. Doc. A/RES/61/295 (Sept. 13, 2007), Article 19

**Legal Reasoning:** Article 19 states: "States shall consult and cooperate in good faith with the Indigenous Peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent before adopting and implementing legislative or administrative measures that may affect them." The textual structure is significant: the operative requirement is to "consult and cooperate in good faith" while "obtain their free, prior and informed consent" describes the objective or purpose of that consultation. The phrase "in order to" suggests that consultation is the means and consent is the goal, not that consent is a mandatory outcome. This textual reading is supported by comparison with Article 10, which states "No relocation shall take place without the free, prior and informed consent" – the mandatory language in Article 10 contrasts with the procedural language in Article 19. The drafting history shows that "consultation" language was deliberately chosen over stronger formulations that would have required consent in all circumstances. The chapeau of UNDRIP also states that rights must be exercised "in accordance with the United Nations Charter and the principles of the Charter, notably the principle of good faith and respect for the territorial integrity and political independence of States."

**Key Quotes:**
- Article 19: "States shall consult and cooperate in good faith with the Indigenous Peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent"
- The operative requirement is to "consult and cooperate in good faith"
- "In order to obtain" suggests consent is the objective of consultation, not a mandatory outcome
- Contrast with Article 10: "No relocation shall take place without the free, prior and informed consent"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** Close textual analysis of Article 19 supports the procedural interpretation adopted by states, ILO supervisory bodies, and most legal commentators, distinguishing it from situations where UNDRIP uses mandatory consent language.

## 4. ILO CONVENTION 169 INTERPRETATION

### 4.1 ILO, "The concept of free, prior and informed consent, in the ILO Convention"

**Citation:** International Labour Organization, "The concept of free, prior and informed consent, in the ILO Convention," Available at: https://www.un.org/esa/socdev/unpfii/documents/workshop_FPIC_ILO.doc

**Legal Reasoning:** This official ILO document clarifies that ILO Convention 169 establishes consultation as the general requirement, with consent required only in specific exceptional circumstances. The document explains that Articles 6 and 7 of Convention 169 establish the framework for consultation and participation, which must be undertaken "in good faith and in a form appropriate to the circumstances, with the objective of achieving agreement or consent to the proposed measures." The ILO clarifies that consent is explicitly required only for relocation (Article 16) and is implied in other specific situations like authorization of storage of hazardous materials (Article 15). The document emphasizes that "Convention No. 169 uses terms such as the obligation to 'cooperate' with indigenous peoples; the obligation not to take measures contrary to the 'freely-expressed wishes' of indigenous peoples; and the obligation to seek 'free and informed consent' of indigenous peoples where 'relocation…is considered necessary.'" This shows Convention 169 uses a spectrum of requirements tailored to different situations, with general consultation being the baseline and consent reserved for circumstances with the most severe impacts.

**Key Quotes:**
- Articles 6 and 7 establish consultation "in good faith and in a form appropriate to the circumstances, with the objective of achieving agreement or consent"
- "Consent is explicitly required only for relocation (Article 16)"
- "Convention No. 169 uses terms such as the obligation to 'cooperate' with indigenous peoples; the obligation not to take measures contrary to the 'freely-expressed wishes' of indigenous peoples; and the obligation to seek 'free and informed consent' of indigenous peoples where 'relocation…is considered necessary'"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** As the authoritative interpretation of the only binding international treaty specifically on Indigenous rights, this ILO guidance establishes the baseline that international law distinguishes between consultation (general requirement) and consent (exceptional requirement for specific high-impact situations).

### 4.2 ILO Convention 169, Articles 6, 15, and 16

**Citation:** International Labour Organization (ILO) Convention concerning Indigenous and Tribal Peoples in Independent Countries (ILO No. 169), June 27, 1989, Available at: https://www.ilo.org/media/458651/download

**Legal Reasoning:** The text of Convention 169 itself demonstrates the distinction between consultation and consent requirements. Article 6(1) establishes the general consultation requirement: "In applying the provisions of this Convention, governments shall: (a) consult the peoples concerned, through appropriate procedures and in particular through their representative institutions, whenever consideration is being given to legislative or administrative measures which may affect them directly; (b) establish means by which these peoples can freely participate, to at least the same extent as other sectors of the population, at all levels of decision-making." Article 6(2) requires consultations to be "undertaken, in good faith and in a form appropriate to the circumstances, with the objective of achieving agreement or consent to the proposed measures." Note that consent is an "objective" not a requirement. In contrast, Article 16(2) contains mandatory consent language: "Where the relocation of these peoples is considered necessary as an exceptional measure, such relocation shall take place only with their free and informed consent." Article 15(2) similarly provides: "In cases in which the State retains the ownership of mineral or sub-surface resources or rights to other resources pertaining to lands, governments shall establish or maintain procedures through which they shall consult these peoples, with a view to ascertaining whether and to what degree their interests would be prejudiced, before undertaking or permitting any programmes for the exploration or exploitation of such resources pertaining to their lands." The textual differences are deliberate: general measures require consultation with the objective of consent; relocation requires actual consent; resource exploitation requires consultation to ascertain impacts.

**Key Quotes:**
- Article 6(1): "Governments shall consult the peoples concerned, through appropriate procedures...whenever consideration is being given to legislative or administrative measures which may affect them directly"
- Article 6(2): Consultations must be "undertaken, in good faith...with the objective of achieving agreement or consent"
- Article 16(2): "Where the relocation of these peoples is considered necessary as an exceptional measure, such relocation shall take place only with their free and informed consent"
- Article 15(2): Resource exploitation requires consultation "with a view to ascertaining whether and to what degree their interests would be prejudiced"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The textual structure of Convention 169 clearly establishes a hierarchy: consultation is the general requirement; consent is mandatory only for relocation; other provisions create varied obligations tailored to context. This textual framework supports interpreting FPIC in international law as primarily procedural.

### 5.4 Proportionality and the Duty to Consult (Canada)

**Citation:** Government of Canada, "Aboriginal Consultation and Accommodation - Updated Guidelines for Federal Officials to Fulfill the Duty to Consult," March 2011, Available at: https://www.rcaanc-cirnac.gc.ca/eng/1100100014664/1609421824729

**Legal Reasoning:** These federal guidelines explain how Canada implements proportionality in consultation obligations. The guidelines establish that "the scope of the duty to consult varies with the circumstances and is proportionate to the preliminary assessment of the strength of the claim and the severity of the potential adverse impact on the right." Following the Supreme Court's Haida Nation decision, the guidelines create a spectrum: "Where the claim to title is weak, the Aboriginal right limited or the potential for infringement minor, the Crown's only duty may be to give notice, disclose information and discuss any issues raised." However, "where a strong prima facie case for the claim is established, the right and potential infringement is of high significance to the Indigenous groups and the risk of non-compensable damage is high, 'deep consultation' may be required." Critically, even deep consultation "does not provide Indigenous groups with a veto right over development projects, though the process requires meaningful engagement with their concerns." The guidelines explain that at the accommodation stage, "the rights of the Indigenous groups must be balanced with other societal interests" through proportionality analysis.

**Key Quotes:**
- "The scope of the duty to consult varies with the circumstances and is proportionate to the preliminary assessment of the strength of the claim and the severity of the potential adverse impact"
- "Where the claim to title is weak, the Aboriginal right limited or the potential for infringement minor, the Crown's only duty may be to give notice, disclose information and discuss any issues"
- "Where a strong prima facie case for the claim is established...deep consultation may be required"
- "The duty to consult does not provide Indigenous groups with a veto right over development projects"
- "The rights of the Indigenous groups must be balanced with other societal interests"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** Canada's proportionality framework aligns with international approaches under Convention 169 and Inter-American Court jurisprudence that calibrate consultation intensity to impact severity while maintaining that consultation is a procedural requirement, not a veto power.

### 5.5 R. v. Sparrow: Justified Infringement Test (Canada)

**Citation:** R. v. Sparrow, [1990] 1 S.C.R. 1075 (Supreme Court of Canada)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Sparrow decision established the framework for justifying infringements of Aboriginal rights in Canada, which has influenced international thinking on balancing Indigenous rights with broader public interests. The Court established a two-stage test: First, has there been a prima facie infringement of an Aboriginal right? If so, is the infringement justified? For justification, the government must show: (1) a valid legislative objective (conservation and resource management, public safety are valid objectives); (2) the government's fiduciary duty to Aboriginal peoples has been upheld; (3) there has been as little infringement as possible; (4) fair compensation in cases of expropriation; and (5) Aboriginal groups were consulted regarding conservation measures. The Court emphasized that "conservation and natural resource management, as well as public safety, are valid objectives" that can justify infringing Aboriginal rights. However, the Court clarified that "'public interest' is not sufficient because it is too vague and broad a concept to justify limiting a constitutional right." The Sparrow test demonstrates that even constitutionally protected Indigenous rights can be infringed if governments meet consultation requirements and demonstrate compelling public interests with minimal infringement.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Conservation and natural resource management, as well as public safety, are valid objectives" that can justify infringement
- "The 'public interest' is not sufficient because it is too vague and broad a concept to justify limiting a constitutional right"
- For justification, consultation and accommodation of Indigenous interests is required
- The government must show "as little infringement as possible"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Sparrow justification framework demonstrates that even where Indigenous rights are strongest (constitutionally protected), they can be balanced against compelling public interests through proper consultation and minimal infringement, supporting the view that FPIC does not constitute an absolute veto.

### 6.2 New Zealand: Treaty of Waitangi and Resource Management Act

### 6.3 Chile: ILO Convention 169 Implementation and Supreme Court Decisions

### 6.4 Bolivia: Plurinational Constitution and TIPNIS Conflict

**Citation:** "Prior Consultations in Plurinational Bolivia: Democracy, Rights and Real Life Experiences," Latin American and Caribbean Ethnic Studies, Vol. 8, No. 2 (2013), Available at: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17442222.2013.808497

**Legal Reasoning:** Bolivia's 2009 Constitution established the country as a "plurinational state" with strong Indigenous rights protections, including constitutional recognition of Indigenous self-determination and prior consultation. Between June 2007 and February 2012, "27 prior consultations were concluded in Bolivia's hydrocarbon sector." However, implementation revealed "frequent shortcomings of consultation practices included the 'information hurdle', irregularities, the limited decision-making power of affected local populations and the lack of transparency regarding the compensation payments." Both scholars' work demonstrates that international law has not resolved fundamental tensions between Indigenous rights claims and state sovereignty, particularly regarding whether Indigenous peoples have veto power over resource development. Their comparative analysis shows that different legal systems balance Indigenous participation rights against state resource authority in varied ways, but common across systems is the distinction between consultation/participation rights (widely recognized) and veto power (generally not recognized). Their scholarship emphasizes that international law provides procedural protections through consultation while leaving substantive resource decisions to states subject to proportionality and accommodation requirements.

- Both analyze "tensions between Indigenous self-determination aspirations and state sovereignty frameworks"
- Common across systems is "the distinction between consultation/participation rights (widely recognized) and veto power (generally not recognized)"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** This comparative scholarship demonstrates that across multiple legal systems and conceptual frameworks, consultation and participation rights are distinguished from veto power, supporting interpretations that FPIC functions as a procedural safeguard within state sovereignty frameworks.

## 9. ADDITIONAL CASE LAW FROM VARIOUS JURISDICTIONS

### 9.1 Philippines: Indigenous Peoples Rights Act Implementation and Supreme Court Cases

**Citation:** Republic Act No. 8371 (Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997), and related Philippine Supreme Court decisions including La Bugal B'laan v. Secretary of Environment and Cariño v. Insular Government (1909)

**Legal Reasoning:** The Philippines "was the first country to incorporate FPIC into national legislation with the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997." IPRA requires FPIC for activities including "exploration, development, exploitation and utilization of natural resources within ancestral domains/lands; research in indigenous knowledge; displacement and relocation; archeological explorations; and entry of military or paramilitary forces within the domains." The Philippine Supreme Court has addressed IPRA's requirements in several cases. In the landmark 1909 case of Cariño v. Insular Government, "the court recognized long occupancy of land by an Indigenous member as one of private ownership (termed 'native title')." In La Bugal B'laan v. Secretary of Environment, Indigenous groups challenged the Mining Law before the Supreme Court. The Court held that "IPRA's granting indigenous communities ownership rights of natural resources 'was interpreted narrowly...as not 'granting' ownership rights over subsoil resources, as to do so was contrary to the Regalian doctrine" (state ownership of natural resources). Despite IPRA's strong FPIC provisions, implementation has faced challenges: "In some instances, IP communities allege they were never properly consulted, or that consent was obtained through coercion or misinformation," and "the principle of 'free, prior and informed consent' enshrined in IPRA has been used by external forces to divide communities."

**Key Quotes:**
- The Philippines "was the first country to incorporate FPIC into national legislation with the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997"
- IPRA requires FPIC for numerous activities including resource exploitation, relocation, and research
- The Supreme Court held that IPRA "was interpreted narrowly...as not 'granting' ownership rights over subsoil resources, as to do so was contrary to the Regalian doctrine"
- "The principle of 'free, prior and informed consent' enshrined in IPRA has been used by external forces to divide communities"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Philippine experience demonstrates that even where FPIC is incorporated into national legislation with apparently strong protections, courts and implementation bodies interpret these provisions in ways that balance Indigenous rights against state resource sovereignty, consistent with understanding FPIC as a procedural safeguard rather than absolute veto.

### 9.2 Finland and Nordic Countries: UN Committee Decisions on Sami Rights

**Citation:** UN Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (CESCR), decision on Finland (2024); UN Human Rights Committee decisions on Finland (2019); Various Sami rights cases

**Legal Reasoning:** The Sami people, "the only indigenous people in the European Union," inhabit "Sápmi, which today covers the northern parts of Norway, Sweden, Finland and the Kola Peninsula in Russia." Multiple UN human rights committees have found Nordic countries violated Sami rights through inadequate consultation. "In October 2024, the United Nations Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (UNCESCR) issued a landmark decision stating that Finland had violated the rights of the Sámi Indigenous people to their culture and land." The Committee "concluded the Sámi have a right to their lands, territories and resources – and that the Finnish state had violated these rights by not consulting them." Earlier, "in 2019, the United Nations Human Rights Committee found in two decisions that Finland violated the rights of the Sámi under the International Covenant in Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)." These decisions criticize consultation failures but frame remedies in procedural terms. "Sweden and Finland...has received massive criticism from the international community on the treatment of their indigenous population. In particular, the critique has concerned Sweden's and Finland's disregard for the right to free, prior informed consent." The European Court of Human Rights has been less receptive to Sami claims, with the 1983 case G and E vs Norway setting aside Sami claims because "the nomadic lifestyle of the Sámi was incompatible with 'traditional' property rights concepts."

**Key Quotes:**
- "In October 2024, the UNCESCR issued a landmark decision stating that Finland had violated the rights of the Sámi Indigenous people"
- The Committee "concluded the Sámi have a right to their lands, territories and resources – and that the Finnish state had violated these rights by not consulting them"
- "In 2019, the United Nations Human Rights Committee found...that Finland violated the rights of the Sámi under...the ICCPR"
- "Sweden and Finland...has received massive criticism...concerning...disregard for the right to free, prior informed consent"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** UN Committee findings against Nordic countries demonstrate that violations are framed primarily as consultation failures requiring improved procedural protections, rather than establishing Indigenous veto power. The emphasis is on requiring adequate consultation processes to protect substantive rights.

### 9.3 Argentina: Salinas Grandes Lithium Mining Case

**Citation:** Salinas Grandes communities v. Argentina, pending before Inter-American Court of Human Rights; Supreme Court of Argentina proceedings (2010-present)

**Legal Reasoning:** Indigenous communities from Salinas Grandes in Argentina's Jujuy province have been challenging lithium mining projects without adequate consultation since 2010. "In November 2010, 33 communities from Salinas Grandes filed a lawsuit at the National Supreme Court in Buenos Aires asking for the cancellation of all mining permits granted without any Free, Prior and Informed Consent." Argentina "adopted ILO Convention 169 in 1992, which requires governments to conduct free, prior and informed consultations" and "in 1994, Argentina's constitutional reform recognised the ethnic and cultural pre-existence of indigenous peoples." However, "companies did not conduct an adequate free, prior and informed consultation (FPIC) with affected communities before beginning to explore for lithium on their ancestral land." The case has proceeded slowly through Argentina's courts. "The United Nations special rapporteur on Indigenous rights, James Anaya, visited the communities surrounding the salt flats and issued a report in 2013. In March 2023, the Supreme Court requested a report from the state." The case is "currently before the Inter-American Court of Human Rights (IACHR)." While awaiting final resolution, "the Catamarca Court set a precedent that could be applied to the ecosystem damage suffered by the provinces of Salta and Jujuy."

**Key Quotes:**
- "In November 2010, 33 communities from Salinas Grandes filed a lawsuit at the National Supreme Court in Buenos Aires asking for the cancellation of all mining permits granted without any Free, Prior and Informed Consent"
- Argentina "adopted ILO Convention 169 in 1992" and "in 1994, Argentina's constitutional reform recognised the ethnic and cultural pre-existence of indigenous peoples"
- "Companies did not conduct an adequate free, prior and informed consultation (FPIC) with affected communities"
- The case is "currently before the Inter-American Court of Human Rights"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The Argentina litigation demonstrates that even where Convention 169 is binding and constitutional recognition exists, obtaining remedies for consultation failures is difficult and protracted, and remedies focus on requiring consultation rather than automatically canceling projects, supporting procedural interpretations of FPIC.

### 9.4 Greenland: Self-Government and Extractive Industries

**Citation:** Greenland Self-Government Act (2009) and related mining consultation cases

**Legal Reasoning:** Greenland's case is unique because "the Self-Government Act allows the government of Greenland a way of de facto Inuit government" and "after 2009 the governance of the subsurface mineral resources has been the responsibility of the Self-government." The "Inuit in Greenland, who since 2009 hold extensive self-government authority, consider extractivism inseparable from the agenda toward greater political autonomy." This creates an unusual situation where the Indigenous people exercise governmental authority over resources. "Danish and Greenlandic governments have taken the view that the Self-Government Act of Greenland from 2009 constitutes full implementation of UNDRIP, since it includes matters on extractive industries, and few government officials found that FPIC is not relevant for Greenland because the Inuit are not a minority." However, implementation challenges remain: "There was an absence of appropriate public participation, consultation, decision-making and regulatory processes and...there were strong local concerns about the impacts of extractive industries." Research found that "both supporters and opponents of the mine project want better information and increased public participation." The debate over uranium mining led to political changes: "Greenland's now largest political party, Inuit Ataqatigiit...had been campaigning on improved welfare and the protection of the environment, which also meant opposing uranium mining."

**Key Quotes:**
- "The Self-Government Act allows the government of Greenland a way of de facto Inuit government"
- "Inuit in Greenland, who since 2009 hold extensive self-government authority, consider extractivism inseparable from the agenda toward greater political autonomy"
- "Danish and Greenlandic governments have taken the view that the Self-Government Act...constitutes full implementation of UNDRIP"
- "There was an absence of appropriate public participation, consultation, decision-making and regulatory processes"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** Greenland demonstrates that even where Indigenous peoples exercise governmental authority, debates about consultation and participation in specific projects continue, suggesting that FPIC requirements function as ongoing participatory processes rather than single consent determinations, supporting procedural interpretations.

### 9.5 Alaska: ANCSA and Indigenous Consultation in Oil Development

**Citation:** Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA), 43 U.S.C. §§ 1601-1629h (1971); Various Alaska consultation cases

**Legal Reasoning:** The Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA), "signed into law by President Richard Nixon on December 18, 1971, constitut[es] what is still the largest land claims settlement in United States history." However, "ANCSA extinguished Indigenous claims to nearly 400 million acres of land throughout the state in exchange for $962.5 million and ownership of 43.7 million acres." Significantly, "ANCSA took land from jurisdictional control of Alaska Natives, allowing the petroleum industry and State of Alaska to gain access to oil reserves." The Act was driven by oil development interests: "In 1968, the Atlantic-Richfield Company discovered oil at Prudhoe Bay on the Arctic coast, catapulting the issue of land ownership into headlines." ANCSA's approach contrasts with FPIC requirements: "ANCSA extinguished aboriginal claims to land and 'any aboriginal hunting and fishing rights that may exist.'" Some Alaska Natives have argued that "ANCSA has hastened cultural genocide of Alaska Natives" and critiqued it "as an illegitimate treaty since only tribal leaders were involved and the provisions of the act were not voted on by indigenous populations." Recent Arctic development continues to raise consultation concerns: "Further consultation never materialized...North Slope tribes, cities, ANCSA corporations...were blindsided by an administration's decision" and "The Inupiat should have been consulted and should have further input on rule changes."

**Key Quotes:**
- ANCSA "constitutes what is still the largest land claims settlement in United States history"
- "ANCSA extinguished Indigenous claims to nearly 400 million acres of land throughout the state"
- "ANCSA took land from jurisdictional control of Alaska Natives, allowing the petroleum industry and State of Alaska to gain access to oil reserves"
- "ANCSA extinguished aboriginal claims to land and 'any aboriginal hunting and fishing rights that may exist'"

**Alignment with International Jurisprudence:** The ANCSA example demonstrates the U.S. approach of settling Indigenous claims through extinguishment and compensation rather than ongoing consultation requirements, representing a more restrictive approach than international standards. However, recent consultation gaps in Alaska illustrate ongoing tensions about Indigenous participation even under this framework.

## 10. SYNTHESIS AND CONCLUSION

The comprehensive research demonstrates substantial legal authority supporting the interpretation that **FPIC is fundamentally a procedural duty to consult in good faith, rather than a substantive right conferring veto power over development projects.** Key findings include:

**International Treaty Law:** ILO Convention 169, the only binding international treaty specifically on Indigenous rights, explicitly distinguishes between consultation (the general requirement) and consent (required only for relocation). The ILO's authoritative supervisory body, the CEACR, has consistently interpreted the Convention as not providing veto rights.

**International Court Decisions:** The Inter-American Court of Human Rights in Saramaka and Sarayaku established that consultation is the baseline requirement, with consent required only for "large-scale" projects with "major impacts." Even in these cases, the Court emphasized robust consultation processes rather than absolute Indigenous control.

**Domestic Constitutional Courts:** Courts in Canada, Brazil, India, South Africa, Norway, and other jurisdictions have interpreted consultation duties as procedural requirements that can be satisfied through good faith processes, after which development may proceed with appropriate accommodations even without Indigenous consent.

**UN Bodies:** The UN Special Rapporteur, Expert Mechanism, Permanent Forum, Human Rights Committee, and CESCR have emphasized robust consultation requirements while acknowledging state sovereignty and resource development authority. Remedies for consultation failures focus on improving processes rather than granting veto power.

**UNDRIP Textual Analysis:** Article 19's language—"consult and cooperate in good faith...in order to obtain" FPIC—uses procedural terminology where consultation is the operative requirement and consent is the objective. This contrasts with Article 10's mandatory consent language for relocation.

**State Practice:** Implementation of UNDRIP and Convention 169 by states consistently interprets FPIC as requiring meaningful consultation processes aimed at securing consent, not as requiring consent as a prerequisite to state action. The Canadian government's position during UNDRIP implementation is representative: "the emphasis is not on the consent or achieving the consent, it's in the process of working towards achieving consent."

**Balancing Framework:** International law requires balancing Indigenous rights against state sovereignty, public interest, and the rights of others. Courts have established proportionality tests and justification frameworks allowing infringement of Indigenous rights where compelling state interests are demonstrated and proper consultation has occurred.

**Corporate and Financial Institution Standards:** IFC Performance Standard 7, World Bank ESS7, and ICMM position statements interpret FPIC as requiring good faith negotiation processes in specified high-impact circumstances, treating consent as the preferred outcome of negotiation rather than an absolute prerequisite.

This body of law and practice supports legal arguments that FPIC constitutes a robust procedural safeguard designed to protect Indigenous substantive rights through meaningful participation, consultation, and accommodation, rather than conferring absolute veto power that would impair state sovereignty and governance authority.
