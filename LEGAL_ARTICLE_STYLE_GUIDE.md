# Legal Article Style Guide
## Academic Legal Journal Submission Standards

This guide provides comprehensive formatting and style requirements for legal academic articles, particularly for submission to law journals following OSCOLA citation standards.

---

## 1. FORMATTING REQUIREMENTS

### 1.1 Document Specifications

- **Word Count**: 5,000 - 10,000 words (inclusive of footnotes)
- **Line Spacing**: 1.5 lines
- **Font**: Times New Roman
- **Font Size**: 12pt
- **Margins**: 1 inch (2.54 cm) on all sides
- **Alignment**: Justified text
- **Page Numbers**: Bottom center, starting from first page of text

### 1.2 Typography Rules

✅ **DO:**
- Use en dashes (–) for ranges: "2015–2024"
- Use spaced hyphens ( - ) for parenthetical clauses
- Use proper quotation marks: "double quotes" and 'single quotes'
- Use footnotes for all citations

❌ **DO NOT:**
- Use em dashes (—) - replace with en dashes or spaced hyphens
- Use endnotes - all citations must be footnotes
- Use decorative fonts or multiple font families
- Use excessive formatting (bold, italics, underline)

### 1.3 Structure Requirements

**Required Elements:**
1. Title (centered, bold, 14-16pt)
2. Author name(s) (centered)
3. Affiliation (centered, italics)
4. Abstract (150-250 words)
5. Keywords (4-6 keywords)
6. Main body text
7. Footnotes (at bottom of each page)

**Optional Elements:**
- Acknowledgments (before main text or in footnote)
- Appendices (if necessary)
- Tables and figures (properly labeled)

## 2. CITATION STYLE: OSCOLA 4TH EDITION

### 2.1 General Principles

**OSCOLA (Oxford University Standard for Citation of Legal Authorities)** is the standard citation system for UK legal academic writing and increasingly adopted internationally.

**Key Features:**
- Footnote citations (not in-text citations)
- No bibliography unless required
- Minimal punctuation
- Specific formats for different source types

### 2.2 Cases

#### UK Cases

**Format:** *Case Name* [Year] Court Citation [Pinpoint]

**Examples:**
- *R v Smith* [2010] EWCA Crim 1234
- *Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562 (HL)
- *Attorney General v Blake* [2001] 1 AC 268 [45]

#### Indian Cases

**Format:** *Case Name* (Year) Volume Reporter Page (Court)

**Examples:**
- *State of Punjab v Gurmit Singh* (1996) 2 SCC 384
- *Bharwada Bhoginbhai v State of Gujarat* (1983) 4 SCC 302
- *Rao Harnarain Singh v State* AIR 1958 All 220

**Subsequent References:**
- First reference: Full citation
- Second reference: *Short Name* (n 1) [pinpoint] where "1" is the first footnote number
- Immediately following: ibid [pinpoint]

### 2.3 Legislation

#### UK Legislation

**Format:** Short Title Year, provision

**Examples:**
- Human Rights Act 1998, s 3
- Constitutional Reform Act 2005, sch 2
- European Convention on Human Rights, art 10

#### Indian Legislation

**Format:** Short Title, Year (India), provision

**Examples:**
- Indian Penal Code, 1860 (India), s 375
- Constitution of India, art 21
- Criminal Procedure Code, 1973 (India), s 154

### 2.4 Books

**Format:** Author, *Title* (Edition, Publisher Year) Pinpoint

**Examples:**
- Alison L Young, *Parliamentary Sovereignty and the Human Rights Act* (Hart Publishing 2009) 45
- HLA Hart, *The Concept of Law* (3rd edn, Oxford University Press 2012) ch 5
- Upendra Baxi and others, *The Indian Legal System* (Oxford University Press 2017) 234

**Author Formatting:**
- One author: Full name
- Two authors: "John Smith and Jane Doe"
- Three authors: "John Smith, Jane Doe and Bob Jones"
- Four+ authors: "John Smith and others"

### 2.5 Journal Articles

**Format:** Author, 'Title' (Year) Volume(Issue) Journal First Page, Pinpoint

**Examples:**
- John Smith, 'Consent and Autonomy' (2020) 45(2) Legal Studies 123, 125
- Jane Doe and Bob Jones, 'Translation and Law' (2019) 72 Current Legal Problems 456
- Upendra Baxi, 'Access to Justice' (1985) 23 JILI 69

### 2.6 Online Sources

**Format:** Author, 'Title' (Website, Date) <URL> accessed Date

**Examples:**
- UN Human Rights Committee, 'General Comment No. 34' (2011) <http://www2.ohchr.org/english/bodies/hrc/docs/gc34.pdf> accessed 15 January 2023
- Supreme Court of India, 'Annual Report 2023' (SCI, 2023) <https://main.sci.gov.in> accessed 20 March 2024

### 2.7 Ibid and Cross-References

**Ibid Rules:**
- Use "ibid" when citing the immediately preceding source
- Same pinpoint: "ibid"
- Different pinpoint: "ibid 45"

**Cross-References:**
- Use "(n X)" where X is the footnote number
- Example: "As discussed in *Smith* (n 5) [45], the test requires..."

## 3. WRITING STYLE

### 3.1 Academic Legal Writing Standards

**Clarity and Precision:**
- Use clear, direct language
- Avoid jargon unless necessary
- Define specialized terms on first use
- Use active voice when possible

**Analytical Depth:**
- Make arguments, not just descriptions
- Support claims with evidence
- Engage with counter-arguments
- Show contemporary relevance

**Structure:**
- Logical progression of ideas
- Clear section headings
- Signpost transitions between sections
- Conclude each section before moving to next

### 3.2 Common Phrases and Structures

**Study famous legal papers for language patterns:**

**Introducing Arguments:**
- "This article argues that..."
- "The central thesis of this article is..."
- "This article contributes to the literature by..."
- "This article demonstrates that..."

**Engaging with Literature:**
- "As X has argued..."
- "Contrary to Y's claim..."
- "Building on Z's framework..."
- "The prevailing view suggests... However..."

**Analytical Transitions:**
- "This raises the question of..."
- "It follows that..."
- "The implications of this are..."
- "Crucially, this demonstrates..."

**Presenting Evidence:**
- "The empirical evidence suggests..."
- "Analysis of X cases reveals..."
- "The data demonstrates..."
- "Systematic examination shows..."

**Concluding:**
- "This article has demonstrated..."
- "The findings suggest that..."
- "The implications for legal policy are..."
- "Further research is needed on..."

### 3.3 Words and Phrases to Avoid

❌ Avoid:
- "Very", "really", "quite" (weak modifiers)
- "Obviously", "clearly", "certainly" (unless truly obvious)
- "I think", "I believe" (too personal for academic writing)
- Contractions ("don't", "can't")
- Informal language ("a lot of", "kind of")
- Em dashes (—) - use en dashes (–) or hyphens instead

✅ Prefer:
- "Demonstrates", "establishes", "reveals"
- "Substantially", "significantly", "materially"
- "The evidence suggests", "The analysis shows"
- Formal constructions
- Precise quantification
- Spaced hyphens or en dashes

## 4. STRUCTURE AND ORGANIZATION

### 4.1 Standard Article Structure

#### **Title**
- Clear, specific, engaging
- Indicates scope and argument
- Avoid overly broad titles
- 10-20 words optimal

#### **Abstract** (150-250 words)
- Problem statement
- Research question or thesis
- Methodology (if empirical)
- Key findings
- Implications

#### **Introduction** (500-800 words)
- Context and motivation
- Research question/thesis
- Brief literature review
- Roadmap of article
- Contribution to scholarship

#### **Literature Review / Background** (1000-1500 words)
- Existing scholarship
- Theoretical frameworks
- Gaps in current knowledge
- Position your contribution

#### **Methodology** (500-1000 words, if empirical)
- Data sources
- Analytical framework
- Scope and limitations
- Justification of approach

#### **Analysis** (3000-5000 words)
- Main argument developed
- Evidence presented
- Counter-arguments addressed
- Subsections with clear headings

#### **Implications / Discussion** (500-1000 words)
- Theoretical implications
- Policy recommendations
- Limitations acknowledged
- Future research directions

#### **Conclusion** (300-500 words)
- Restate thesis
- Summarize key findings
- Final reflections
- Broader significance

### 4.2 Section Numbering

**Option 1: Numbered sections**
```
1. INTRODUCTION
2. LEGAL FRAMEWORK
   2.1 Historical Development
   2.2 Current Doctrine
3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK
```

**Option 2: Unnumbered headings**
```
INTRODUCTION
LEGAL FRAMEWORK
   Historical Development
   Current Doctrine
ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK
```

Both are acceptable. Be consistent throughout.

## 5. CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE

### 5.1 Demonstrating Contemporary Relevance

**Legal articles must show:**
- Why the issue matters NOW
- Recent developments that create urgency
- Real-world implications
- Policy relevance

**Strategies:**
- Reference recent cases (last 5-10 years)
- Discuss recent legislative changes
- Connect to current debates
- Show practical significance

### 5.2 Analytical Engagement

**Not sufficient:**
- Pure description of law
- Summarizing existing scholarship
- Historical overview without analysis

**Required:**
- Original argument or thesis
- Critical analysis of existing law
- Proposal for reform or reinterpretation
- Empirical evidence (if applicable)
- Theoretical contribution

## 6. FAMOUS LEGAL PAPERS - LANGUAGE PATTERNS

### 6.1 Exemplary Phrases from Leading Articles

**From HLA Hart, "Positivism and the Separation of Law and Morals":**
- "In this article I shall argue..."
- "This is a necessary, not a sufficient condition..."
- "The fallacy lies in..."

**From Ronald Dworkin, "Hard Cases":**
- "I propose to examine..."
- "This principle explains..."
- "The distinction turns on..."

**From Carol Gilligan, "In a Different Voice":**
- "The findings suggest..."
- "This research demonstrates..."
- "The implications extend to..."

### 6.2 Structure from Leading Law Reviews

**Harvard Law Review style:**
- Roman numeral sections (I, II, III)
- Letter subsections (A, B, C)
- Number sub-subsections (1, 2, 3)
- Extensive footnotes with analytical content

**Modern Legal Studies style:**
- Numbered sections (1, 2, 3)
- Decimal subsections (1.1, 1.2)
- Descriptive headings
- Footnotes for citations only

## 7. FOOTNOTES

### 7.1 Footnote Best Practices

**Content:**
- ✅ Citations to sources
- ✅ Brief additional explanations
- ✅ References to related points
- ❌ Long substantive arguments (put in main text)
- ❌ Tangential discussions

**Format:**
- Number footnotes consecutively (1, 2, 3...)
- Place footnote markers AFTER punctuation
- Example: "This is disputed.¹"
- Not: "This is disputed¹."

**Placement:**
- At the bottom of each page (not as endnotes)
- Single-spaced footnotes acceptable
- Smaller font (10pt) acceptable

### 7.2 When to Cite

**Always cite when:**
- Quoting directly
- Paraphrasing someone's idea
- Using empirical data
- Making controversial claims
- Referencing cases or statutes

**Generally cite:**
- Established legal principles (to authority)
- Scholarly arguments (to source)
- Factual claims (to evidence)

**No citation needed:**
- Common knowledge
- Your own analysis
- Logical deductions from cited premises

## 8. WORD COUNT MANAGEMENT

### 8.1 Meeting the 5,000-10,000 Word Range

**If too short (< 5,000 words):**
- Expand literature review
- Add comparative analysis
- Develop counter-arguments
- Include more examples
- Deepen theoretical discussion
- Add policy implications section

**If too long (> 10,000 words):**
- Remove tangential discussions
- Condense literature review
- Move methodological details to footnotes
- Eliminate redundancy
- Tighten introduction and conclusion
- Focus on core argument

### 8.2 What Counts in Word Count

**Included:**
- Main text
- Footnotes (all text)
- Headings
- Block quotes

**Excluded:**
- Title
- Author name and affiliation
- Abstract
- Keywords
- Bibliography (if separate)
- Appendices

## 11. COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### 11.1 Citation Errors

❌ **Wrong:**
- Using endnotes instead of footnotes
- Inconsistent citation format
- Missing pinpoint citations
- Using "supra" (not OSCOLA style)

✅ **Correct:**
- Footnotes at bottom of page
- Consistent OSCOLA format
- Pinpoint to specific pages/paragraphs
- Use "ibid" or "(n X)" for cross-references

### 11.2 Formatting Errors

❌ **Wrong:**
- Em dashes everywhere
- Mixed fonts
- Single spacing
- Endnotes

✅ **Correct:**
- En dashes or spaced hyphens
- Times New Roman throughout
- 1.5 line spacing
- Footnotes only

### 11.3 Writing Errors

❌ **Wrong:**
- Purely descriptive (no argument)
- No contemporary relevance
- Superficial analysis
- Missing counter-arguments

✅ **Correct:**
- Clear analytical thesis
- Shows current importance
- Deep engagement with issues
- Addresses opposing views
